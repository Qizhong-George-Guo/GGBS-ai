Green Infrastructure

The Green dimension of the GGBS Framework includes vegetation-based and nature-based systems that infiltrate, store, slow, and treat water while providing multiple co-benefits such as cooling, habitat, and improved urban space.

Role of Green Infrastructure in GGBS

Green infrastructure emphasizes the use of soils, vegetation, and landscape features to manage water more like a natural system. It complements conventional drainage and flood-protection works by reducing runoff volume, slowing peak flows, enhancing infiltration, and improving water quality.

Within the GGBS Framework, green infrastructure:

  • Intercepts and stores rainfall before it becomes runoff;
  • Promotes infiltration and groundwater recharge where appropriate;
  • Filters sediments and pollutants through soil and vegetation;
  • Reduces heat stress and improves microclimates;
  • Creates habitat and enhances urban amenity.

Examples of Green Infrastructure Measures

The green dimension covers a range of site-scale and neighborhood-scale practices, including:

  • Rain gardens and bioretention systems that capture and infiltrate roof or street runoff;
  • Green roofs that retain rainfall, reduce peak flows, and provide thermal benefits;
  • Bioswales and vegetated channels that slow and treat road or parking-lot runoff;
  • Permeable pavements that allow water to infiltrate through surfaces such as parking lots and low-speed streets;
  • Urban trees and pocket parks that intercept rainfall and provide shading;
  • Constructed wetlands and riparian buffers that treat water and support ecosystems at larger scales.
In dense urban areas, individual practices are often small, but their cumulative effect across a watershed or district can be significant when planned as part of an integrated GGBS strategy.

Hydrologic and Hydraulic Functions

From a hydrologic and hydraulic perspective, green infrastructure acts on multiple components of the rainfall–runoff process:

  • Initial abstraction (interception, depression storage, infiltration);
  • Runoff attenuation and temporary storage;
  • Baseflow or delayed return flows in some settings;
  • Modification of peak timing and peak magnitude at the catchment scale.

These functions can be represented in models ranging from simple design tools to fully distributed hydrologic–hydraulic simulations. Calibration and field data remain important for realistic performance estimates.

Co-Benefits Beyond Hydrology

Green infrastructure is often justified not only for flood and water quality benefits, but also for a broader set of co-benefits:

  • Urban cooling and heat-island mitigation;
  • Improved air quality and shading for pedestrians;
  • Enhanced aesthetics and recreational space;
  • Habitat creation and biodiversity support;
  • Potential increases in property value and neighborhood quality.

In GGBS, these co-benefits are recognized as part of the system-scale value of green measures, especially when compared with purely grey alternatives.

Integration with Grey and Blue Systems

Green infrastructure rarely operates in isolation. Its full value is realized when it is deliberately integrated with grey and blue systems:

  • Upstream green measures can reduce the size or operating demands of downstream pipes, tunnels, or storage tanks;
  • Roadside bioswales and permeable pavements can connect to conventional storm sewers, improving water quality at outfalls;
  • Urban green networks can be aligned with downstream rivers, lakes, or wetlands to support baseflow and water quality objectives.

GGBS provides a framework for co-designing these interactions rather than treating each system separately.

Planning and Design Considerations

Effective green infrastructure planning typically requires attention to:

  • Site-scale feasibility (soils, slopes, utilities, land availability);
  • Maintenance responsibilities and long-term performance;
  • Climate conditions and extreme events (including drought and heat);
  • Equity and distribution of benefits across neighborhoods;
  • Integration with land-use planning and community priorities.

At the system level, green infrastructure can be located and sized to complement grey capacity and to achieve specific performance targets for flows, levels, or pollutant loads.

Green Infrastructure in Practice

In practice, green infrastructure has been applied in a wide variety of contexts, including:

  • Street-scale retrofits in older urban neighborhoods;
  • New developments designed with low-impact or sponge-city principles;
  • Campus, park, and institutional landscapes;
  • Watershed-scale strategies that combine urban green measures with wetlands and riparian restoration.
