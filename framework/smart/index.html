Smart & Intelligent Infrastructure

The Smart dimension of the GGBS Framework brings together monitoring, modeling, data analytics, and AI-driven decision support to make green, grey, and blue infrastructure more observable, predictable, and adaptive.

Role of the Smart Dimension in GGBS

While green, grey, and blue infrastructure represent physical systems, the smart dimension represents the information layer that sits on top of them. It includes sensing, data management, models, and algorithms that help us understand system behavior, test alternatives, and operate infrastructure in more adaptive ways.

In the GGBS Framework, smart infrastructure:

  • Generates data about how green, grey, and blue systems perform;
  • Supports forecasting of floods, water levels, waves, and water quality;
  • Enables early warning and real-time adjustments to operations;
  • Supports scenario analysis for planning and design under uncertainty;
  • Links physical infrastructure with institutional decision-making.

Smart Infrastructure and Intelligent Systems

In GGBS, “smart” and “intelligent” refer to the combination of infrastructure that can be monitored and controlled, together with the analytical tools that support decisions. This includes both digital technologies and the workflows that use them.

Smart and intelligent systems within GGBS include:

  • Intelligent infrastructure: systems that can be monitored in real time and adjusted in response to changing conditions;
  • Intelligent decision support: tools that help engineers, operators, and decision-makers explore trade-offs and prioritize actions.
In practice, the smart dimension is not only about technology; it is about how information is used to support resilient, equitable, and sustainable decisions across green, grey, and blue systems.

Key Components of Smart / Intelligent Infrastructure

The smart dimension can be broken down into several interacting components:

  • Monitoring networks: rain gauges, water-level sensors, flow meters, water-quality probes, wave buoys, remote sensing, and citizen-science observations.
  • Data platforms: databases, dashboards, and cloud services that store, curate, and visualize data from multiple sources.
  • Models and simulators: hydrologic, hydraulic, groundwater, sediment-transport, and coastal models that represent system behavior across scales.
  • Analytics and optimization: tools for calibration, sensitivity analysis, uncertainty assessment, and optimization of operations or design.
  • AI and machine learning: methods for pattern recognition, forecasting, anomaly detection, and scenario ranking based on large or complex data sets.
  • Decision-support interfaces: interactive dashboards, planning tools, and model-based workflows that connect technical analyses with policy and management choices.

These components do not replace engineering judgment or local knowledge. Instead, they are intended to enhance them.

Applications Across Green, Grey, and Blue Systems

Smart and intelligent systems are relevant wherever decisions depend on changing hydrologic and hydraulic conditions. Examples include:

  • Urban stormwater: real-time control of detention basins, green infrastructure, and pump stations based on rainfall forecasts and observed flows.
  • Watershed and river-basin planning: model-based analysis of reservoir operations, levee systems, land-use change, and nature-based solutions at large scales.
  • Estuaries and coasts: forecasting of tides, storm surge, waves, and compound flooding to support warnings and emergency response.
  • Water quality and ecology: using data and models to evaluate nutrient loads, sediment dynamics, and habitat conditions under different scenarios.

Digital Twins and Adaptive Management

As monitoring and modeling capabilities grow, the idea of a digital twin—a dynamic digital representation of a physical system—becomes more attainable for cities, watersheds, and coasts. In a GGBS context, digital twins can:

  • Integrate green, grey, and blue infrastructure into a single virtual system;
  • Visualize current and projected conditions;
  • Test “what-if” scenarios before implementing changes on the ground;
  • Support adaptive pathways planning under deep uncertainty.

The smart dimension thus plays a central role in moving from static designs toward continuous, learning-based management of infrastructure systems.

Smart GGBS as a Bridge Between Science and Decision-Making

One of the motivations behind GGBS is to help bridge the gap between scientific understanding, engineering practice, and real-world decision-making. The smart dimension is where this bridge is often built:

  • Scientific models are translated into decision-relevant tools;
  • Monitoring data are turned into information and insight;
  • Operational rules are tested and refined based on evidence;
  • Multiple objectives—safety, cost, ecology, equity—can be explored.

In teaching, research, and practice, this dimension offers many opportunities for collaboration among engineers, data scientists, planners, and policy experts.
