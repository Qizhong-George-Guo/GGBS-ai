Grey Infrastructure

The Grey dimension of the GGBS Framework includes engineered and structural systems that convey, store, regulate, or protect against water. These systems provide capacity and robustness, and remain essential components of resilient urban, watershed, and coastal infrastructure.

Role of Grey Infrastructure in GGBS

Grey infrastructure comprises the conventional engineered systems that move and control water through pipes, channels, storage structures, and protective works. It is often designed to meet regulatory standards for capacity, safety, and reliability, especially under extreme events.

Within the GGBS Framework, grey infrastructure:

  • Provides conveyance for stormwater, wastewater, and river flows;
  • Offers storage and attenuation through tanks, basins, tunnels, and reservoirs;
  • Protects communities from floods and coastal hazards via levees, floodwalls, and barriers;
  • Supports navigation, water supply, and hydropower generation in some settings.

Typical Grey Infrastructure Systems

Examples of grey systems considered in GGBS include:

  • Storm sewers and culverts that drain streets and developed areas;
  • Combined or separate sewer systems in older urban cores;
  • Detention and retention basins, tanks, and deep tunnels for temporary storage;
  • Levees and floodwalls that protect floodplains and urban areas;
  • Dams and reservoirs used for flood control, water supply, and flow regulation;
  • Coastal structures such as seawalls, revetments, groins, breakwaters, and surge barriers;
  • Pumps, gates, and control structures that manage water levels and flows.
Many of these systems are already in place and represent large sunk investments. GGBS emphasizes how to operate, retrofit, and complement them with green, blue, and smart measures.

Design, Safety, and Performance

Grey infrastructure is typically designed using hydrologic and hydraulic analyses, with attention to design storms, return periods, structural reliability, and safety factors. Key considerations include:

  • Hydrologic inputs (rainfall intensity, duration, frequency);
  • Hydraulic capacity (pipe/channel sizes, slopes, roughness, backwater effects);
  • Structural design of walls, embankments, and foundations;
  • Design for extreme events, failure modes, and residual risk;
  • Operation rules for gates, pumps, and reservoirs.

As climate and land-use conditions change, the adequacy of existing grey systems needs to be revisited through updated analysis and monitoring.

Limitations and Opportunities

Traditional grey infrastructure can be very effective for specific design conditions, but it has limitations:

  • High capital and maintenance costs;
  • Finite capacity that may be exceeded under rare or changing conditions;
  • Limited co-benefits beyond the primary function;
  • Potential environmental impacts if not well integrated with ecosystems.

Within GGBS, these limitations become opportunities to:

  • Combine grey systems with green measures to reduce loads;
  • Use blue systems (rivers, floodplains, wetlands) as part of the solution;
  • Apply smart/information systems to operate grey infrastructure more adaptively.

Integration with Green, Blue, and Smart Dimensions

The strength of GGBS lies in the deliberate integration of grey infrastructure with other dimensions. Examples include:

  • Using upstream green measures to reduce peak flows entering downstream pipes and tunnels;
  • Designing storage tunnels and tanks that also support water-quality goals by capturing first-flush or combined sewer overflows;
  • Aligning levees and floodwalls with restored floodplains or wetlands to create hybrid blue–grey systems;
  • Equipping key grey assets with sensors, controls, and models to enable smart, real-time operation.

Grey infrastructure thus remains central, but it is no longer viewed in isolation.

Lifecycle, Retrofit, and Equity Considerations

Many communities face decisions about whether to build new infrastructure, retrofit existing systems, or change operations. GGBS encourages a lifecycle perspective that considers:

  • Condition assessment and maintenance needs;
  • Retrofit options that add green or smart components;
  • Adaptation pathways as climate and land use evolve;
  • Distribution of benefits and residual risks across neighborhoods;
  • Opportunities to improve equity and resilience simultaneously.

Grey assets are long-lived; integrating them thoughtfully with green, blue, and smart measures can significantly extend their value.

Grey Infrastructure in Practice

In practice, grey infrastructure remains a backbone of flood protection, drainage, and coastal defense systems worldwide. Examples highlighted in the Applications section include:

  • Urban drainage networks in older and newer developments;
  • Major levee and barrier systems for riverine and coastal floods;
  • Deep tunnels and storage projects in large cities;
  • Hybrid coastal protections that combine seawalls with nourished beaches or dunes.
