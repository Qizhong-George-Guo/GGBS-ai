Blue Infrastructure

The Blue dimension of the GGBS Framework encompasses rivers, lakes, reservoirs, wetlands, estuaries, and coasts. These water bodies convey flow and sediment, store floodwaters, support ecosystems, and provide critical services to society.

Role of Blue Infrastructure in GGBS

Blue infrastructure represents the natural and managed water bodies that form the backbone of hydrologic and hydraulic systems. They are not just “receivers” of runoff; they are active, dynamic components that shape flood behavior, sediment transport, water quality, and ecological conditions.

Within the GGBS Framework, blue infrastructure:

  • Conveys water and sediment from headwaters to estuaries and coasts;
  • Provides temporary or long-term storage of floodwaters;
  • Supports aquatic and riparian ecosystems;
  • Offers navigation, recreation, and cultural values;
  • Connects upstream catchments with downstream coastal zones.

Types of Blue Systems

The blue dimension includes a variety of interconnected systems:

  • Rivers and streams with their channels and floodplains;
  • Natural lakes and reservoirs used for storage and regulation;
  • Wetlands and floodplain ponds that attenuate flows and improve water quality;
  • Estuaries and tidal rivers where fresh and salt water mix;
  • Coastal zones including beaches, dunes, marshes, and nearshore waters.
Many of these systems are modified by dams, levees, channels, ports, and other works. GGBS highlights both the natural dynamics and the engineered modifications that influence how they function.

Hydrodynamics, Sediment, and Water Quality

Blue infrastructure is governed by hydrodynamics and sediment processes, which influence:

  • Flow regimes (low flows, bankfull flows, floods, tides, storm surge);
  • Water levels and inundation extent along rivers and coasts;
  • Sediment erosion, transport, and deposition patterns;
  • Nutrient and pollutant transport, transformation, and residence times;
  • Habitat availability for aquatic and riparian species.

Understanding these processes, and how they respond to climate and land-use changes, is central to designing resilient GGBS strategies.

Flood Storage and Risk Reduction

Blue infrastructure plays a crucial role in flood behavior:

  • Floodplains and wetlands can store and slowly release floodwaters;
  • Reservoirs can regulate peak flows when operated for flood control;
  • Channels, estuaries, and coastal basins shape how floods propagate and combine with tides or surge;
  • Coastal wetlands, dunes, and barrier systems can attenuate waves and water levels.

GGBS encourages the use of blue systems as part of flood-risk management, in combination with grey structures and green measures.

Integration with Green and Grey

Blue infrastructure is tightly coupled with both green and grey systems:

  • Urban green infrastructure ultimately drains to streams, rivers, or coastal waters, affecting flow regimes and water quality;
  • Grey systems such as sewers, storm drains, levees, and outfalls determine how and where water enters blue systems;
  • River and coastal morphology can be influenced by both upstream land-use change and downstream engineering works.

Designing with GGBS in mind means treating rivers, wetlands, and coasts as assets to be worked with, not just constraints to be contained.

Integration with Smart Systems

The smart dimension of GGBS enhances our ability to understand and manage blue infrastructure:

  • Monitoring networks track river levels, wave heights, and salinity;
  • Hydrodynamic models simulate tides, storm surge, waves, and flow routing;
  • Forecast systems support early warning for riverine, estuarine, and coastal floods;
  • Data and models inform reservoir operations, gate settings, and emergency response.

Together, blue and smart dimensions support adaptive management of floods, droughts, sediment, and water quality.

Blue Infrastructure in Practice

In practice, blue infrastructure is central to many of the case studies and applications highlighted in the GGBS Framework:

  • River-basin planning and floodplain management;
  • Reservoir and dam operations for multiple objectives;
  • Wetland and floodplain restoration for flood and water-quality benefits;
  • Estuarine management under sea-level rise and changing freshwater inflows;
  • Coastal protection strategies that combine dunes, beaches, marshes, and grey structures.
