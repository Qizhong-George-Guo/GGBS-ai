About GGBS

What is the GGBS framework?

The GGBS framework (Green–Grey–Blue–Smart) is a systems-based way of thinking about water and infrastructure. It recognizes that modern cities and regions require:

  • Green infrastructure and nature-based solutions,
  • Grey engineered works and utilities,
  • Blue open water and storage systems, and
  • Smart sensing, modelling, and operational tools

to be planned, evaluated, and operated together, not as separate silos. This integrated perspective supports better decisions under climate change, rapid urbanization, and increasing social and economic constraints.

This site will gradually share concepts, case studies, teaching materials, and decision-support tools that illustrate and apply the GGBS framework.

Framework

The four dimensions of GGBS

Green & Grey

Green infrastructure (GI) includes green roofs, rain gardens, permeable pavements, bioswales, and other nature-based or nature-enhancing measures that store, slow, and treat water while co-delivering ecological and social benefits.

Grey infrastructure covers conventional engineered systems—sewers, culverts, tunnels, detention basins, levees, dams, and coastal defenses—that provide capacity, reliability, and robust control.

GGBS emphasizes co-design of green and grey systems so they complement rather than compete with each other.

Blue & Smart

Blue infrastructure represents rivers, lakes, wetlands, estuaries, coasts, and constructed storage (ponds, reservoirs) that regulate flows, support ecosystems, and offer space for floodwater and sediment.

Smart infrastructure leverages monitoring networks, data platforms, numerical models, optimization, and increasingly AI-based analytics to support real-time operations and long-term planning.

Together, GGBS connects physical assets with data and decision-making, across scales from sites and neighborhoods to watersheds and river basins.

Resilience Manage pluvial, fluvial, and coastal flood risks under changing climate and land use.
Sustainability Reduce resource use, energy consumption, and environmental impacts across asset lifecycles.
Equity Consider who benefits, who bears risk, and how investments can reduce social and spatial disparities.
Applications

Applications & case studies

The GGBS framework is being developed and tested across multiple contexts, including:

  • Urban stormwater and “sponge cities” – coupling green infrastructure with conventional drainage systems to reduce flooding and improve water quality.
  • Watershed and river-basin management – integrating land use, reservoirs, levees, and nature-based measures to improve basin-scale resilience.
  • Coastal and estuarine systems – combining hard coastal structures with soft/green and hybrid measures under sea-level rise.

Future updates will showcase selected case studies and modelling tools as they are prepared for wider sharing.
